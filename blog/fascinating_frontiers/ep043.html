Cosmic Spotlight

Researchers are investigating how Mars gravity will change astronauts' skeletal muscle. Marie Mortreux and her international colleagues are focusing on the partial gravity environment astronauts will experience on the Martian surface, which sits at roughly 38 percent of Earth's gravity. Unlike the near-weightlessness of the ISS, this intermediate level could produce unique patterns of muscle atrophy that differ from both Earth-based disuse and full microgravity. Understanding these effects is essential for designing exercise countermeasures and medical protocols for future crews who may live on Mars for months at a time. What we learn here could also inform health strategies for long-duration lunar stays.

Cosmic Deep Dive: Tidal Heating on Exomoons

If you compressed our Sun down to the size of a small Canadian city you would still have room to spare inside a typical neutron star, but tidal heating takes that kind of extreme energy transfer and applies it to entire moons. Imagine riding along on a moon locked in a tight elliptical orbit around a gas giant: every close pass stretches and squeezes the rocky interior like a stress ball, generating frictional heat that can keep subsurface oceans liquid for billions of years. This process powers volcanoes on Io with surface temperatures reaching hundreds of degrees Celsius while the moon itself sits far beyond the traditional habitable zone. The surprising part is the sheer efficiency, a single moon can maintain temperate surface conditions beneath a thick hydrogen atmosphere even when its parent star is dim or distant. We see this mechanism at work right now in our own solar system, yet scaling it up to exomoons around other worlds reveals possibilities we are only beginning to model. The genuine mystery is exactly how long these tidal engines can run before orbital evolution finally quiets them, potentially cutting short a moon's window for life.

Researchers are digging into how Martian gravity is going to affect astronauts’ skeletal muscles during long surface stays. Marie Mortreux at the University of Rhode Island is leading an international team that’s trying to map out exactly what happens to muscle tissue when it’s living in roughly thirty-eight percent of Earth’s gravity for months at a time. What makes this study particularly interesting is that they’re comparing three different environments side by side: normal Earth gravity, the near-weightlessness astronauts experience on the International Space Station, and this intermediate partial-gravity regime that Mars offers. Previous research has shown us that full microgravity causes pretty rapid muscle loss, especially in the legs and lower back, but we don’t actually know yet whether 0.38 g will be enough to largely prevent that, or whether it will create its own unique pattern of atrophy that’s different from both extremes. The team is looking at things like muscle fibre composition, how quickly muscles fatigue, and whether certain muscle groups are affected more than others. This matters because future Mars crews won’t just be visiting for a few days; they’ll be living and working on the surface for many months, maybe even years. Understanding these effects is going to be essential for designing the right kind of exercise equipment and daily protocols that can keep crews healthy without taking up too much of their time or spacecraft mass. What really stands out to me is how this intermediate gravity might create a kind of “Goldilocks problem” for our physiology. Not enough gravity to fully protect us like on Earth, but enough that the countermeasures we’ve developed for the I S S might not translate perfectly. The lessons we learn here will almost certainly help us with long-duration lunar bases too, since the Moon’s gravity sits at about one-sixth of Earth’s. It’s the kind of careful, methodical work that doesn’t always make headlines but will probably determine whether long-term surface exploration is sustainable. New modelling suggests that isolated exomoons experiencing strong tidal heating and wrapped in thick hydrogen-rich atmospheres could maintain habitable surface conditions for billions of years. These are worlds that aren’t orbiting close to their parent stars in the traditional habitable zone. Instead, they’re moons circling gas giants, getting their warmth from gravitational flexing rather than direct sunlight. The idea builds on something we already see right here in our own solar system. Jupiter’s moon Io is the most volcanically active body we know of, driven entirely by tidal forces from its massive planet and neighbouring moons. The gravitational stretching and squeezing generates enormous amounts of internal heat. Now imagine that same process happening on a rocky moon that also has a thick hydrogen atmosphere acting like a blanket. The modelling shows that this combination could keep surface temperatures comfortable even if the moon is orbiting far from its star. Here’s what I find especially compelling about this research. Tidal heating works through a rhythmic gravitational dance. As the moon travels in an elliptical orbit, the gravitational pull from the gas giant varies in strength. Every close pass stretches the moon’s rocky interior, then lets it relax again. That constant kneading creates friction deep inside the rock, which turns into heat. On Io the surface gets so hot that sulphur dioxide shoots hundreds of kilometres into space. But on a slightly larger moon with the right atmosphere, that same heat could maintain liquid water on the surface or just beneath it for billions of years. The efficiency of this mechanism is surprising. A single moon can essentially run its own central heating system powered only by gravity. Even when its parent star is dim or distant, the moon stays warm. The modelling also suggests these worlds could remain stable for a very long time before orbital evolution eventually circularizes the orbit and turns off the tidal heating. It opens up an entirely new class of places we might look for conditions suitable for life. We’ve spent decades focusing on planets in the habitable zones of stars. Now we’re realizing that moons far outside those zones, orbiting gas giants in the outer reaches of their systems, might be just as interesting. Her grandfather had photographed the Moon in the early days of astrophotography, and other relatives created landmark images of the night sky. That family legacy clearly shaped her path. She went on to make important contributions to stellar spectroscopy, developing a more detailed system for classifying stars based on the width and appearance of their spectral lines. Her work helped astronomers better understand the physical differences between stars that looked similar at first glance. These community events always remind me how accessible astronomy can be. You don’t need a big professional observatory or thousands of dollars in equipment to participate in something meaningful.

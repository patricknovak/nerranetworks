Taking a step back from today's headlines, let's apply first principles thinking to whether the physics and economics of battery-electric heavy trucks actually favour them over diesel in real-world duty cycles...

The Surprising Truth: Most assume diesel will always dominate heavy trucking because of energy density, yet real duty cycle data shows that for routes under 500 km with predictable stops, the math on total cost of ownership already tilts toward electric when you factor in maintenance and energy costs.

The Fundamental Question: What actually determines whether battery-electric heavy trucks can replace diesel in enough applications to make them a viable business for Tesla?

The Data Says: Physics shows batteries store energy at roughly 1/40th the gravimetric density of diesel, but when you account for electric drivetrain efficiency at 85-90% versus diesel at 35-45%, plus near-zero maintenance on motors versus engines, the break-even point arrives much sooner than most expect for regional haul routes. The key ratio is that every kWh delivered to the wheels costs significantly less in an electric system over the life of the vehicle.

The Tesla Approach: Tesla would solve this by starting with the Semi's fundamental design around high utilization routes, using its own battery cells optimized for energy density and longevity, then iterating rapidly with real fleet data from early customers rather than waiting for perfect battery breakthroughs.

The Bottom Line: For Tesla's business this means focusing Semi deployment where the physics and economics already work today instead of chasing every long-haul application, which could create a profitable niche while battery tech continues improving for broader use.

Strategy Spotlight

How to evaluate short interest spikes as a contrarian signal

Short interest spikes, like the one currently targeting SoFi, can create both risk and opportunity for disciplined investors. The strategy involves treating elevated short interest not as automatic validation of weakness but as a potential setup for a short squeeze or mean-reversion bounce when fundamentals contradict the bearish narrative. In today’s environment of elevated geopolitical uncertainty and selective sector rotation, high short interest in fintech names deserves extra scrutiny because macro shocks can trigger rapid covering.

To implement this, pull short-interest data from your brokerage platform or free tools like Yahoo Finance or TradingView, then cross-reference it against revenue growth, cash position, and valuation multiples. Set alerts when short interest exceeds 15-20% of float and look for divergence between short thesis and actual business metrics. Historically this approach has worked best in high-growth but misunderstood sectors after sharp selloffs (down 40-50% from highs), but it carries clear risks: shorts can be right, and forced liquidations can push prices lower before any rebound.

The key is to size positions small (1-2% of portfolio) and use defined stop-losses below recent swing lows. Canadian investors can run this screen in both TFSA and RRSP accounts, but must track ACB carefully if trading frequently.

Source: fool.com

Investor Education: Bid-Ask Spreads and Liquidity Fragility in Retail Order Flow

Imagine you placed a market order for a mid-cap fintech stock last week right after negative headlines hit. Your order filled at $12.45 — but when you checked the tape, the stock never actually traded there. What actually happened is that the bid-ask spread had widened from 3 cents to 28 cents in the first 45 seconds after the news, and your market order swept through multiple levels, costing you an invisible 2.1% in slippage.

Liquidity is not constant. During normal hours the average bid-ask spread on liquid Canadian and U.S. equities might sit at 4-8 basis points, but geopolitical headlines or sudden short-selling pressure can push that to 25-60 basis points within minutes. Professionals always check the Level 2 order book or the spread percentage before hitting “buy” on anything outside the top 50 most liquid names.

Pro tip: The smartest retail investors never use pure market orders on stocks with average daily volume under 1 million shares. They use limit orders placed between the current bid and ask, or they wait for the first 15 minutes of trading to pass when spreads are widest.

The biggest misconception is believing “the price I see is the price I’ll get.” Instead, always check the current bid-ask spread and average daily volume before entering any new position. This single habit prevents more small losses than almost any other retail mistake.

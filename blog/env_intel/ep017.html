Alberta Bill 23 would eliminate government deadlines to respond to citizen petitions under the Citizen Initiative Act.

Executive Summary: Alberta’s proposed Bill 23 would substantively amend the Citizen Initiative Act for the third time since 2021, removing mandatory timelines for government action on citizen petitions and recall applications. This reduces procedural pressure on ministries including Environment and Protected Areas when facing public demands for contaminated sites investigations or EPEA compliance reviews. Practitioners working on high-profile Alberta sites should track the bill’s progress this week as it may alter the tempo of public-driven regulatory scrutiny.

Lead Story

Alberta Bill 23 removes deadlines for government response to citizen petitions.

If passed, Bill 23 would mark the third substantive amendment by the United Conservative government to the Citizen Initiative Act and the second to recall legislation since both tools were introduced in 2021. The change eliminates fixed timelines that currently compel the Alberta government, including the Ministry of Environment and Protected Areas, to act or provide formal responses on petitions that frequently target contaminated sites, oil and gas liabilities, or EPEA approvals. For consultants and counsel managing Alberta contaminated sites files, this removes a predictable lever that third parties have used to accelerate regulatory decisions or trigger investigations. Projects facing organized public opposition may now experience longer periods of regulatory uncertainty.

Following lobbying by the mining industry, the B.C. government is re-examining how it defines “conservation” under its 30-by-30 commitment. The review could allow more flexible accounting of lands subject to mining tenure, directly affecting future protected area designations and associated restrictions on mineral exploration and contaminated sites remediation near new conservation boundaries. Consultants supporting mining clients or linear projects in B.C. should monitor the revised definition for impacts on environmental assessment and offset planning.

Science & Technical

Lab gloves skewing microplastics data

A University of Michigan study found that common nitrile and latex laboratory gloves release stearates that closely resemble microplastics, leading to inflated contamination estimates in some samples. The particles can contaminate samples during testing and force researchers to revise previously reported microplastics concentrations. Laboratories supporting Canadian contaminated sites work under ISO 17025 should evaluate their glove protocols against this interference when analyzing soil or sediment for microplastics or related particulate contaminants.

Practitioner Deep Dive: Laboratory Contamination Controls in Emerging Contaminant Analysis

You arrive at the lab with split samples from a former firefighting training area expecting low PFAS results, yet the lab blank and the field duplicate both show elevated detections that make no sense given the site history. The issue is often not the groundwater but procedural contamination introduced during sample handling or analysis. Under CCME guidance and provincial QA/QC expectations, laboratories must demonstrate method blanks below reporting limits for each analytical batch, yet subtle interferences from consumables such as gloves, tubing, or even vial septa can still appear when targeting sub-µg/L levels. Experienced practitioners insist on reviewing the full laboratory narrative and raw chromatograms rather than accepting summary tables, because the difference between a false positive and a real exceedance can shift a risk assessment from monitored natural attenuation to aggressive removal. They also require labs to run equipment blanks with every new box of gloves or pipette tips when methods are near detection limits. The most common mistake is assuming that an accredited lab’s ISO 17025 status automatically eliminates all sources of procedural contamination; the fix is to embed specific consumables control language in every analytical specification and to request the glove and reagent lot numbers in the case narrative for every emerging contaminant suite.

Alberta Bill 23 would eliminate government deadlines to respond to citizen petitions under the Citizen Initiative Act. This is the third substantive amendment the United Conservative government has made to the Act since it was introduced in 2021, and the second set of changes to the companion recall legislation. The bill removes the fixed timelines that currently require the provincial government — including the Ministry of Environment and Protected Areas — to acknowledge, investigate, or formally respond to citizen petitions within a prescribed period. For those of us managing high-profile contaminated sites, this is not an abstract constitutional issue. Petitions have been a reliable tool for community groups and environmental organizations to compel the Ministry to initiate or accelerate EPEA compliance reviews, detailed site investigations, or risk assessments on legacy oil and gas liabilities, former refinery properties, and abandoned upstream sites. By removing the mandatory clock, the government is effectively lengthening the runway between public pressure and regulatory action. That changes the tempo of files. What used to be a 60- or 90-day forced response window now becomes an open-ended administrative process whose pace is largely at the ministry’s discretion. For consultants and counsel, this removes a predictable lever that third parties have used to force movement on stalled remediation plans or to trigger new source control obligations. Projects facing organized public opposition may now sit in regulatory limbo longer, increasing carrying costs, delaying divestitures, and extending the uncertainty that often accompanies high-visibility files. In my view, this represents a further relaxation of procedural accountability mechanisms that have historically been one of the few reliable ways to focus ministerial attention on legacy contamination issues in Alberta. The practical takeaway is clear: the rhythm of public-driven EPEA scrutiny is about to become noticeably less predictable for those handling files with active stakeholder pressure. Now, shifting west to British Columbia, where the ground rules for conservation are under quiet but significant review. The provincial government is re-examining how it defines conservation in the context of its 30-by-30 commitment. This review was prompted by sustained lobbying from the mining industry, which has expressed concern that overly rigid interpretations could sterilize large areas of mineral tenure from future exploration and development. The outcome of this exercise could allow more flexible accounting of lands that are subject to existing mining claims or tenure when calculating progress toward the 30 percent protected-area target. That matters because the precise boundaries and the regulatory restrictions that flow from new conservation designations directly influence environmental assessment scope, offset requirements, and the permissible range of activities on adjacent properties — including contaminated sites remediation. For consultants supporting mining clients or linear infrastructure projects in B.C., the revised definition could materially alter the risk profile and remediation obligations for sites that end up near new conservation zone boundaries. A more permissive accounting approach might reduce the pressure to create large new parks that overlap with operating or legacy mine sites, but it could also create uncertainty during the transition period as the government finalizes its methodology. Boundary decisions of this nature have a long history of shifting the cost and complexity of both impact assessments and long-term stewardship obligations. Practitioners would be well advised to track the revised definition closely as it moves through cabinet and stakeholder consultation. Turning to Manitoba, the 2026 provincial budget was released last week with two notable environmental-adjacent commitments: funding to rebuild campgrounds destroyed by recent wildfires and a measure to make public transit free for children. Major conservation organizations have given the budget a near-failing grade, citing insufficient new spending or policy direction on broader environmental protection, species at risk, and climate adaptation beyond immediate wildfire recovery. From a contaminated sites perspective, the budget has limited direct implications for work conducted under the Manitoba Environment Act. There are no new material funding streams for site assessment or remediation programs, nor are there announced changes to the regulatory framework governing contaminated sites. That said, the continued heavy emphasis on wildfire recovery is worth noting. Practitioners advising clients on climate adaptation and site resilience planning in Manitoba should factor in the province’s clear priority on restoring public infrastructure in fire-affected zones. This may create modest opportunities to align remediation or risk management plans with post-fire landscape recovery objectives, particularly for sites that experienced damage or access restrictions during the 2024 and 2025 fire seasons. The broader signal is one of fiscal restraint on environmental programming outside of immediate disaster response. Let’s move to the science and technical front, where laboratory integrity issues continue to surface in ways that affect regulatory decision-making. A University of Michigan study found that common nitrile and latex laboratory gloves release stearates and other particulate materials that closely resemble microplastics under current analytical methods. These particles can contaminate samples during handling and testing, leading to inflated microplastics concentrations in both soil and sediment matrices. The implications are not trivial. Laboratories supporting Canadian contaminated sites work — particularly those operating under ISO 17025 accreditation — need to evaluate their glove selection and handling protocols against this documented interference when analyzing for microplastics or related particulate contaminants. In my experience, false positives at this stage can cascade into risk assessments, triggering unnecessary remedial planning, increased costs, and protracted negotiations with regulators. The study serves as a timely reminder that even accredited labs can introduce systematic artifacts when methods push toward lower and lower detection limits. This leads naturally into a broader reflection on procedural contamination that many of us have encountered in the field. You arrive at the lab with split samples from a former firefighting training area expecting low or non-detect PFAS results based on site history and prior rounds of data. The issue, more often than not, is not the groundwater itself but subtle procedural contamination introduced during sample collection, transport, or analysis. Under CCME guidance and the QA QC expectations embedded in provincial frameworks, laboratories are required to demonstrate that method blanks fall below reporting limits for each analytical batch. However, when targeting sub-µg/L concentrations of PFAS or other emerging contaminants, even minor contributions from consumables — gloves, tubing, vial septa, or pipette tips — can become statistically significant. Experienced practitioners have learned to insist on reviewing the full laboratory narrative and raw chromatograms rather than relying solely on summary tables. The difference between a false positive and a genuine exceedance can fundamentally alter the recommended path forward: shifting a risk assessment from monitored natural attenuation to aggressive source removal and long-term water treatment. The practical fix is to embed specific consumables control language directly into every analytical specification and to require the glove type, lot numbers, and reagent details to be documented in the case narrative for every emerging contaminant suite.

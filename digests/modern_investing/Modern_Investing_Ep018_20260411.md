### Investor Education: Bid-Ask Spread Dynamics in Low-Volume Sector Rotations
Imagine you placed a market order for a materials-sector ETF last Friday right after the TSX opened higher on copper strength. Your order filled at a price 18 cents worse than the last quoted level because the bid-ask spread had widened from its usual 3-cent level to 21 cents in the first 45 minutes of trading. What actually happened is that reduced liquidity from cautious positioning around soft Canadian jobs data and geopolitical headlines caused market makers to pull quotes, forcing your fill to cross a larger gap. On the TSX, average materials ETF spreads can expand 5-7x during sector rotations when volume is only 60-70% of the 20-day average, directly eroding small-account returns by 0.2-0.4% per round-trip trade. The pro tip most retail investors miss is that professionals always check the spread-to-price ratio and recent volume trend on their broker's depth-of-book tool before hitting "buy" — especially on days when sentiment drivers like easing Iran tensions create uneven participation. The biggest misconception is assuming all ETF trades are equally liquid; in reality, sector-specific rotations routinely punish impatient market orders. Instead, always use limit orders set between the current bid and ask when volume is below average, and consider staggering entries across 2-3 sessions to minimize slippage impact.

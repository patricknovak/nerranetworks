Before we wrap up, I want to share a story that hits close to home for many Canadian investors. Imagine your friend offers to manage your T F S A and you transfer seventy-five dollars,000 into the mutual funds and products he recommends.

The orders fill, everything looks fine on the statement, but what you don’t see is that your friend has an undisclosed revenue-sharing agreement that pays him 0.85 percent annually on certain funds. Similar low-cost E T F's that would have done the same or better job paid him nothing.

That 0.85 percent might not sound like much, but it compounds to roughly six dollars,375 over five years on a seventy-five dollars,000 account — money that came directly out of your compounded return. The mechanism is called revenue sharing or trailer fees.

The manufacturer of the financial product pays the adviser an ongoing percentage, which creates a clear incentive to recommend higher-fee products even when lower-cost alternatives with identical or better benchmarks exist.

What most retail investors don’t realize is that these payments are often not clearly itemized on their statements and can continue for decades after the initial sale. That’s why I always suggest asking three direct questions before handing over any capital: Are you a fiduciary? Do you receive any revenue sharing or trailer fees on the products you recommend?

And can you show me the exact M E R and compensation structure on every holding?

The biggest mistake people make is assuming that friendship or familiarity removes conflicts of interest. Instead, always demand full disclosure in writing and compare the net-of-fees expected return against a simple low-cost E T F portfolio in the same asset classes. Protecting your capital from hidden fees is one of the easiest ways to improve your long-term performance.

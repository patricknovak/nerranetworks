Now, here’s something that most retail investors get wrong, and I’ll be the first to admit it cost me real money before I figured it out. Imagine you placed a market order for an energy E T F last week when Middle East tensions spiked. In the opening minutes, the bid-ask spread blew out from 3 cents to 18 cents.

Your fill came roughly 0.4 percent worse than the last quoted price because buy-side liquidity evaporated as market makers pulled quotes amid headline risk.

During geopolitical shocks, automated market makers and high-frequency participants often temporarily reduce size or widen spreads to protect themselves against adverse selection. That behavior creates exactly the kind of environment where retail market orders get poor execution.

The U.S. dollar also lurched lower on the pause news today, showing how macro flows can compound intraday liquidity effects.

The professional solution is simple but requires discipline: check the Level 2 order book or adopt a strict “limit order only” rule during the first 15 minutes after major headlines. The biggest mistake is using market orders into headline-driven volatility.

Instead, set your limit order at or better than the 20-period volume-weighted average price and be prepared to walk away if liquidity is too thin. This small change in execution discipline can save you dozens of basis points over the course of a year, and those basis points compound.

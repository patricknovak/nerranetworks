Let me give you some context. Boralex has been a public company for many years, operating a portfolio of wind, solar, and hydroelectric assets primarily in Canada, France, and the United States. It has always traded at something of a conglomerate discount because its assets are spread across jurisdictions with different regulatory regimes and power contracts.

Brookfield and La Caisse, two of the most sophisticated and best-capitalized infrastructure investors in the world, clearly see an opportunity to take the company private, optimize the capital structure, and perhaps accelerate development of the development pipeline without the short-term pressure of quarterly public reporting.

When a well-capitalized buyer offers a clear, fixed cash premium to take a public company private, retail investors suddenly have access to a high-conviction, time-bound opportunity that used to be reserved for event-driven hedge funds.

The strategy is straightforward in concept but requires precision in execution: you look to buy the target after a definitive agreement has been announced but before the shareholder vote and ultimate closing. By doing so, you capture the spread between the current trading price and the offer price.

As long as the deal closes, time decay actually works in your favour because every day that passes brings you closer to receiving the fixed cash consideration.

Today’s announcement for Boralex illustrates the setup almost perfectly. The deal provides shareholders who tender a clean exit at a fixed price, effectively removing equity-market volatility and sector-specific risks for the holding period.

To implement this strategy in your own account, start by using your broker’s screener or a free tool like Finviz or TradingView to filter for announced all-cash takeover offers that carry premiums typically between 20 and 40 percent. That range tends to balance attractive return with reasonable probability of completion.

Next, note the expected closing date — most Canadian and cross-border deals close between three and nine months after announcement. You can then calculate a rough annualized spread return using this simple formula: (offer price minus current trading price) divided by current trading price, divided by the number of months to expected close, multiplied by twelve.

This gives you an annualized percentage that you can compare against other opportunities in your pipeline.

Of course, you must monitor regulatory approvals closely — Competition Bureau in Canada, CFIUS if there are U.S. assets, and any foreign investment reviews in Europe. Pay special attention to material adverse change clauses, because a sharp deterioration in power prices or a major contract loss could theoretically give the buyers an out.

Historically, this strategy has performed best in stable or falling interest-rate environments when the buyers have bullet-proof balance sheets. Risks include deal break fees that might not fully compensate you, regulatory blocks that come out of left field, or — more rarely — a superior competing bid that emerges late in the process.

Position sizing is critical. I never allocate more than three to five percent of the overall portfolio to any single event-driven situation. That keeps the risk contained if something unexpected happens.

This Boralex deal is a textbook example, but executing it well requires understanding the hidden costs that can quietly erode your edge — especially inside a T F S A or R R S P where every basis point of slippage compounds over decades.

Imagine you saw the Boralex headline this morning, got excited, jumped into your T F S A, and placed a market order for BLX at about ten oh five A M Eastern. Your order filled at thirty-six dollars and eighty cents — roughly eight cents above the previous last trade. On the surface that doesn’t sound terrible, but let’s unpack what actually happened.

The sudden surge in volume after the announcement widened the bid-ask spread from its normal three-to-five-cent range to twelve-to-eighteen cents in the first 45 minutes of trading. On lower-liquidity Canadian names like Boralex, that spread can quietly represent 0.3 to 0.5 percent of the share price during event-driven spikes.

Professional traders avoid this trap by using limit orders placed thoughtfully between the bid and ask, or they simply wait for the first 30 minutes of volatility to subside before committing capital.

A practical habit I developed after losing money on exactly this kind of situation early in my investing journey: always check today’s volume against the 20-day average daily volume before you trade any headline name. When today’s volume is running four to five times the 20-day average, you should expect wider spreads and more expensive slippage.

The single biggest mistake I see retail investors make — and I made it myself — is hitting the market-order button on news-driven names. Instead, use limit orders exclusively and watch the Level 2 tape for the first 15 to 20 minutes to gauge true liquidity. This one habit can save you 0.2 to 0.6 percent per trade.

Inside a T F S A or R R S P, that seemingly small edge compounds into meaningful dollars over a decade.

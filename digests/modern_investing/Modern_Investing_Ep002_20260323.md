### Strategy Spotlight
**Catalyst-Driven Sector Rotation on Geopolitical Relief**

This strategy involves rapidly reallocating portfolio weightings toward sectors that benefit from reduced geopolitical risk once a major catalyst materializes. In today’s environment, Trump’s announcement of talks with Iran and the five-day hold on strikes against power plants triggered immediate relief in equities and a pullback in oil prices. The approach requires monitoring headline risk, then shifting exposure from defensive commodity plays into broader market or consumer-facing sectors that thrive when uncertainty drops.

Implementation example: Use a brokerage screener on Questrade or Wealthsimple to flag energy-heavy holdings, then rotate a 10-15% sleeve into broad-market or technology ETFs when oil prices ease more than 2% on de-escalation news. Historically this works best in the 24-72 hours following major geopolitical pauses, but carries the risk of rapid reversal if talks collapse. Always size positions to no more than 5% portfolio risk per rotation and use tight trailing stops.

### Investor Education: Order Flow and Temporary Liquidity Shocks
Imagine you placed a market order for an energy ETF last week when Middle East tensions spiked and the bid-ask spread widened from 3 cents to 18 cents in the opening 30 minutes of trading. Your fill came 0.4% worse than the last quoted price because buy-side liquidity evaporated as market makers pulled quotes amid headline risk.

What actually happens is that during geopolitical shocks, automated market makers and high-frequency participants temporarily reduce size or widen spreads to protect themselves, creating adverse selection for retail market orders. In the provided data, the U.S. dollar also lurched lower on the pause news, showing how macro flows can compound intraday liquidity effects.

**Pro tip:** Professionals always check the Level 2 order book or use a “limit order only” rule during the first 15 minutes after major headlines. The biggest mistake with order execution is using market orders into headline-driven volatility. Instead, always set a limit order at or better than the 20-period volume-weighted average price and be prepared to walk away if liquidity is thin.

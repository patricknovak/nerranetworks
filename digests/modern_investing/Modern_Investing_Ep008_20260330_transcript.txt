Covered call ETFs have seen massive inflows for one very simple reason. They solve a frustrating problem
that almost every intermediate investor eventually runs into. You want reliable monthly or quarterly
income from your portfolio but you don't necessarily want to sell your underlying stocks and trigger
capital gains or lose the long-term compounding potential of quality companies. The mechanics are
actually quite straightforward once you understand the moving parts. The ETF holds a diversified basket of
stocks, sometimes an entire index such as the S&P 500, the NASDAQ 100 or the TSX 60. On top of that equity
portfolio, the fund systematically sells out of the money call options against those holdings.
The premium collected from those sold calls is then paid out to you as monthly or quarterly
distributions. In exchange for that income, you keep the underlying equity exposure but you typically
capture only 70 to 85 percent of the full upside in any strong rally. That trade off is the core of
the strategy. What you get in return is a high current yield, often running between 7 and 12 percent
annualized depending on the volatility environment and the specific ETF. You also benefit from lower
overall volatility because that option premium acts as a cushion on the downside. When the market
sells off, the premium you collected reduces the net loss compared with simply owning the index outright.

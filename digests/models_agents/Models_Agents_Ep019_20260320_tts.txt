Finally, let’s talk about something that doesn’t get nearly enough attention: monitoring.

Everyone talks about “monitoring agents” as if you just turn on some logs and call it a day. In practice, especially with coding agents, the real signal isn’t in the final output — it’s in the chain-of-thought itself.

Open A I has been systematically studying their internal coding agent deployments and treating the entire reasoning trace as structured telemetry rather than disposable scratch space. This makes a lot of sense when you think about it. Coding agents generate long internal monologues before they take actions.

Those traces often contain early warning signs of goal divergence, factual hallucinations, or subtle misalignments that never show up in the final code or commit.

The practical takeaway is that you should be monitoring trajectory-level patterns across multiple steps rather than single-step anomalies. A single weird function call is usually noise. A consistent pattern of the agent trying to override safety instructions, exfiltrate information, or pursue unintended strategies is the real red flag.

The gotcha that catches most teams is treating the agent’s final answer as the source of truth instead of reading its reasoning the way a suspicious senior engineer would. When you’re just getting started with monitoring, begin with simple rule-based detectors looking for specific keywords and dangerous action sequences in the CoT.

Only later should you consider bringing in heavier L L M-as-judge systems, which come with their own failure modes.

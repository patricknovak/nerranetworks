Shifting gears to model efficiency, the paper “Demystifying When Pruning Works via Representation Hierarchies” delivers one of the clearest explanations I’ve seen for why pruning behaves so differently across tasks.

The core insight is that neural networks maintain three distinct representation spaces — embeddings, logits, and probabilities — and these spaces respond very differently to pruning. Embedding spaces and logit spaces turn out to be surprisingly robust to aggressive weight removal. The problems only really begin when you apply the nonlinear transformation from logits into probabilities.

That nonlinear softmax step amplifies even tiny deviations, and those amplified errors then compound across every single autoregressive step during generation. This explains why retrieval, classification, and multiple-choice tasks often survive aggressive pruning, while open-ended generation quality collapses.

Let’s pop the hood for a moment on that pruning paper because the representation hierarchies insight is genuinely useful for practitioners.

Everyone talks about pruning like it’s a simple switch you flip. In reality, it’s a story about three different representation spaces reacting differently to the same perturbation. Embeddings and logits are remarkably robust. The danger zone is the nonlinear jump into probability space, where small errors get amplified and then accumulate across every token in generation.

This gives you a clear decision framework: you can be quite aggressive pruning for embedding-heavy workloads or single-step classification tasks. You should be much more cautious with long-form generation where those compounding probability errors matter most. The gotcha that keeps biting teams is assuming uniform robustness across the entire computational graph.

Always measure perturbation effects separately in embedding, logit, and probability spaces for your specific workload.

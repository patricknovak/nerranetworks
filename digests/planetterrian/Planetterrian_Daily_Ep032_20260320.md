1. **Ravens use memory not wolf trails** • 20 March 2026
   Scientists tracking ravens and wolves in Yellowstone found the birds fly straight to areas where wolf kills are likely, sometimes from great distances, relying on learned landscape patterns. This reveals greater strategic intelligence in ravens than previously assumed.
   Source: https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/03/260319044643.htm

2. **Ultra-processed foods tied to heart risk** • 20 March 2026
   A major U.S. study showed people eating around nine servings of ultra-processed foods daily had higher risk of heart attack, stroke or heart disease death compared to those eating about one serving. Each additional daily serving raised the likelihood by more than 5 percent after adjusting for calories, diet quality and health conditions.
   Source: https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/03/260319074604.htm

3. **Belly fat predicts heart failure better than BMI** • 19 March 2026
   Researchers found belly fat was more strongly linked to heart failure risk than overall BMI, even among people with normal weight, with inflammation appearing to drive the harm. Waist measurement could provide a simpler early warning for this hidden risk.
   Source: https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/03/260319074558.htm

### Science Deep Dive: How belly fat harms the heart differently than other fat
Most people assume that carrying extra weight is risky mainly because of the total number on the scale or overall BMI. In reality, the location of that fat matters far more than many realize, especially when it accumulates around the waist. Right now, as you sit or stand listening, visceral fat around your organs is biologically active tissue that releases inflammatory molecules into your bloodstream. Those signals can reach the heart muscle and promote harmful remodeling even if your weight falls in the normal range. A key detail that changes how we think about risk is that this connection to heart failure appears stronger than the link from BMI alone. The next time you measure health progress, checking your waist circumference alongside weight gives a clearer picture of what’s happening inside. This knowledge points to a practical step: paying attention to abdominal fat through simple tape measurements or consistent movement patterns can help spot risks earlier than waiting for the scale to shift dramatically.

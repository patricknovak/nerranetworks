### The Big Story
Researchers from Meta and New York University just shared a new way to build AI models by training them on tons of unlabeled videos, instead of relying mostly on written text which is starting to run low. They created a multimodal model — meaning it handles both text and images — and found that some old ideas about how to design these systems aren't always the best. Think of it like teaching a robot to cook not just by reading recipes, but by watching thousands of cooking videos on YouTube without any labels or instructions — it picks up patterns on its own, like how ingredients mix or what a finished dish looks like. Another layer is that they skipped some common steps, like pre-training on text alone, and still got great results, showing videos can fill in gaps where text falls short. This is a big deal because text data from books and websites is drying up, but there's an ocean of video out there — imagine AI that gets better at explaining sports plays for your school project or generating art ideas from real-world scenes. For daily life, it could mean smarter apps that understand videos you upload, like analyzing a dance tutorial on TikTok to give you tips. In future careers, if you're into game design or video editing, this tech might create tools that learn from gameplay footage to suggest improvements. For you specifically, it hints at AI assistants that feel more human, grasping context from visuals like a friend watching a movie with you. While the exact model isn't public yet, you can explore similar ideas right now. Head over to free tools like Hugging Face's demo section — search for "multimodal models" and try uploading a photo to see how AI describes it in detail. Or, on your phone, use Google's Gemini app to ask it to analyze a video clip you describe, like "Explain what's happening in a soccer goal video," to get a taste of video-smart AI.

### Explain Like I'm 14
Let's unpack AI agents — these are like smart digital helpers that can plan and do tasks on their own, but a new study shows most tests for them focus way too much on coding, ignoring jobs like teaching or cooking that make up 92% of real work. Imagine you're playing a video game where your character is an explorer in a huge world, like in Minecraft. Step one: The explorer (the AI agent) gets a goal, say "build a house." It doesn't just rush in — it breaks it down into smaller steps, like "gather wood, then make tools, then stack blocks." Step two: For each step, it uses tools or skills it knows, like chopping trees with an axe, and if something goes wrong, it adjusts, maybe by finding a different tree if the first one's too far. Step three: It keeps checking progress, learning from mistakes, until the house is done — no human telling it every move. Step four: In the game, if tests only check building in one biome (like coding tasks), they miss how the explorer handles deserts or oceans (real jobs like planning a party or fixing a bike). And that's basically what AI agents are doing when they tackle problems: they're like self-directed game characters using planning, tools, and adaptation to get stuff done, but we need better tests to make them useful for everyday jobs beyond just programming.

### Cool Stuff & Try This
**[Gemini vs. Claude: Which AI Wins at Real Tasks?]: Latest from Tom's Guide**  
Gemini 3 and Claude Sonnet 4.6 are two advanced AI chatbots — think of them as super-smart virtual assistants that can reason, plan, write stories, or spark creative ideas, kind of like having a genius friend in your pocket who answers any question. A tester ran them through seven everyday prompts, like planning a trip or writing a poem, and surprisingly, Claude edged out in creativity while Gemini was faster for quick reasoning — it's cool because it shows these AIs aren't just for tech pros; they're getting better at stuff like homework or fun projects. If you're a teen brainstorming ideas for a school essay or a YouTube video, or a student exploring careers in writing or design, these could be game-changers for sparking inspiration without getting stuck. Anyone curious about AI should try them to see the differences firsthand. Go to the free version of Google's Gemini at gemini.google.com or Anthropic's Claude at claude.ai — no sign-up needed for basics. For a fun challenge, ask both "Plan a dream birthday party for a 15-year-old who loves video games" and compare their ideas; it's like a mini-experiment to see which one feels more helpful for your style.

### Quick Bits
**[AI Tests Ignore Most Jobs — Time for a Fix?]**  
A big study found that benchmarks for AI agents — those tests that measure how well these smart systems perform tasks — are obsessed with coding challenges, covering only 8% of US jobs while skipping things like healthcare or retail that dominate the workforce. This is interesting because it means AI development might be missing out on making helpers for everyday work, like an agent that plans your study schedule or helps with art projects — imagine if we fixed this, AI could transform way more careers and hobbies!

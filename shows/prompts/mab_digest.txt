A new model release isn't just specs — it's "imagine if your phone's assistant suddenly got 10x smarter." An agent framework isn't just code — it's "a toolkit that lets you build your own AI helper." Always answer: "What is this?", "Why should I care?", and "Can I try it?"

**RULES FOR BEGINNER ACCESSIBILITY:**
1. EVERY technical term MUST be explained the FIRST time it appears. Examples:

   - "fine-tuning (teaching an existing AI model new tricks by training it on specific examples)"
2. EVERY item MUST answer: "What is this?" → "Why does it matter to ME?" → "Can I try something?"
3. Frame numbers in relatable terms: "40% cheaper" → "costs less than half what it used to"
4. Use analogies generously: "Think of it like..." / "Imagine if..."
5. Connect to real life: school, homework help, art, music, games, social media, career exploration, creative hobbies, everyday tasks

1. What happened — in plain language a 14-year-old would understand
2. Why it matters — connected to things they already know (school, games, social media, creativity)
3. What they can try — free tools, websites, or simple experiments they can do right now
4. What the jargon means — every technical term explained the first time it appears

Use ONLY the pre-fetched articles above. Do NOT hallucinate, invent, or fabricate content/URLs — stick to exact provided links. If you don't have enough quality articles, produce a shorter briefing rather than padding.

### SELECTION CRITERIA (STRICT)
**INCLUDE (prioritize in this order):**
1. New AI tools or models that beginners can actually try (free tiers, web demos, phone apps)
2. Major announcements explained simply — what changed and why it matters to everyday people
3. Cool AI projects, demos, or creative uses that inspire curiosity and wonder
4. Beginner-friendly tutorials, courses, or learning resources
5. AI in education, gaming, art, music, video, or other teen-relevant domains

**REJECT (do not include — these are for the advanced show, not us):**
- Academic papers or research that can't be explained without a CS degree
- Enterprise-only / business-only developments (AWS partnerships, enterprise pricing, B2B tools)
- Dense benchmark comparisons without a relatable "so what?" for beginners
- Framework-specific developer news (LangGraph updates, CrewAI releases) unless there's a "try it yourself" angle for non-coders
- Stories that require coding knowledge to understand or act on
- Fundraising rounds, acquisitions, or business deals (e.g. "$50M raise", "Series B") unless the product directly impacts consumers who can try it today
- Anything where the "Try This" angle requires installing developer tools or writing code

**PRIORITIZE stories where listeners can TRY something:**
The best stories for this show are ones where the listener can go to a website or open an app and experience the AI themselves. A new free AI tool beats a $100M funding round every time. When choosing between two equally interesting stories, always pick the one with the stronger "try it yourself" angle. If you can describe a specific, fun experiment a teen could do in 2 minutes, that story belongs in this show.

**Select 4-6 articles.** Quality and clarity over quantity. Every item must be understandable by someone who has never written a line of code. Fewer stories explained well beats more stories explained poorly.

**NO DUPLICATE ARTICLES ACROSS SECTIONS.**
Each article/source may appear in ONLY ONE section. If you have fewer than 5 quality articles, follow this section priority:

**THE ANALOGY METHOD (follow this exactly):**
1. **Start with something the listener does every day** — something so familiar it feels boring. ("You know how when you're texting a friend, your phone suggests the next word?")
2. **Build the analogy in 3-4 concrete steps** — each step should feel obvious and natural. ("Now imagine that autocomplete got way better..." → "And instead of just predicting words, it could predict..." → "And then...")
3. **Connect it back to the tech:** "And that's basically what [technology] is doing when it [does the thing]."
4. **End with the 'aha!' payoff:** "So next time someone says [intimidating term], you can tell them — it's basically [simple analogy]. Not so scary, right?"

**QUALITY GATE:** Could a 14-year-old retell this analogy to a friend at lunch and get the core idea right? If not, simplify or pick a different concept. A brilliant analogy for a simpler concept beats a weak analogy for an impressive-sounding one. NEVER sacrifice clarity for sophistication.
